Gigala — pre-industrial grade software

Topology optimization by reinforcement learning (TO by RL) represents a paradigm shift in structural design: an intelligent agent — powered by a neural network — learns to sequentially place or remove material within a design domain, iteratively refining the layout to achieve optimal performance under given loads, boundary conditions, and constraints. Unlike classical mathematical methods that rely on gradients and assumptions of continuity, RL treats the problem as a game of strategic decisions, enabling the discovery of highly complex, non-intuitive topologies that traditional approaches often miss.

One can think of topology optimization by reinforcement learning as of an intelligent design. You can design machinery’s elements and components or you can design bridges and buildings. But now this approach transforms to something new.

You see, the thing is that TO by RL has a neural network as its component. The neural network makes decision how to assemble a structure step by step in a sequence. But the idea that recently came to my mind was why not to transfer that neural network into physical domain?! Why not to put it, for example, in the brain of a robot?! And now suddenly instead of a design algorithm you have an assembly algorithm. You get what I call agent-technologists and agent-builders who use the ‘brain’ or the neural network obtained by topology optimization with RL to build in physical world.

To elucidate, think of a natural disaster - a hurricane or an earthquake. It damages buildings, roads, factories, shops, etc. However, now, you just give an agent-builder the task to repair the damage. You photo damaged building, put the photo in your computer. It is an initial topology of your structure. You set proper boundary conditions for the algorithm and do TO by RL to suggest repairs so that your structure is strong and light again. After you got the sequence of repair steps, you put the very neural network that made decisions to assemble the topology, in the robot’s brain, and just like that, the robot is ready to repair the building in physical world.Another example would be space exploration. Think of lunar bases, for instance. It is dangerous to send people to assemble the lunar base, but you could send robots who would carry both topology optimization algorithm and assembly algorithms inside their brains. The robot would arrive to the moon, study terrain, choose a place for the base, photo or video record the surroundings, design the structure with TO by RL, and then assemble that structure on the terrain by the sequence of assembly actions provided from reinforcement learning design. The robot (or swarm of robots) would use lunar regolith and other available materials to support the construction of the structure. Hence, agent-builder paradigm could meaningfully contribute to colonization of other worlds (Fig. 1).

Thus, TO by RL excels at multi-objective optimization, delivering structures that simultaneously maximize strength while dramatically minimizing weight — the holy grail for aerospace, automotive, and space applications. It scales naturally to 3D problems via e.g. pseudo 3D approach, adapts to irregular constraints, and, crucially, produces policies that can be transferred directly into the physical world. The same neural network that designs the structure can guide a swarm of robots to actually build it, turning digital optimization into real-world autonomous construction.

This matters because raw material performance alone is not enough. Cutting-edge nanomaterials — no matter how strong or lightweight at the atomic scale — cannot deliver their promise without an intelligent macro- or meso-scale architecture. Even advanced composites fall short if their internal structure is suboptimal; they still require a carefully engineered topology to channel forces, avoid stress concentrations, and achieve the full strength-to-weight potential.

In sum, one can say that topology optimization with reinforcement learning has achieved preindustrial grade where it is able to design simple components like pins, hinges or small elements. And all of this is possible on your personal computer within 1hr wall-clock run time. If in doubt, check my open-source software Gigala, \[3\], and start building today.
